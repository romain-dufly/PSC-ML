Curriculum learning (from easy task to harder task)

[Curriculum learning; Y Bengio, J Louradour, R Collobert, J Weston; ICML 2009]
  • Main idea: not harder "tasks", but harder "examples": pick gradually harder and harder examples, i.e. make the probability of picking hard examples to increase with time
  • Formalization: instead of training directly on the distribution pp of all examples, train on a distribution qtqt varying with time and including gradually harder examples more frequently:
    • at time t∈[0,1]t∈[0,1], consider the distribution qt(z)=wt(z)p(z)qt(z)=wt(z)p(z) over all examples zz
    • with wtwt increasing with tt, for each zz (i.e., starting from few examples and adding some with time)
    • and entropy H(qt)H(qt) increasing with tt (i.e., more and more diversity)
    • and wt=1wt=1 for all zz, i.e. final distribution q1(z)=q1(z)= target p(z)p(z)
  • NB: notion of "easy"/"hard" example = defined by hand by the user
  • Experiments:
    • classifying geometrical shapes (triangles, rectangles...) from easy shapes to more complex ones
    • NLP task with growing vocabulary
      ⟹⟹ notice faster & better convergence

Choosing physically meaningful metrics

Optimal transport (Sinkhorn approximation)

  • [Sinkhorn Distances: Lightspeed Computation of Optimal Transportation Distances; Marco Cuturi; NIPS 2013]
    and in primal form: [Learning Generative Models with Sinkhorn Divergences; Aude Genevay, Gabriel Peyré, Marco Cuturi; AISTATS 2018]
  • Goal: to compare output distribution pp with desired (ground truth) one qq
  • Optimal transport: a distance between distributions, quantifying how much work is needed to transform one distribution into the other one. Analogy: moving earth with a shelve, minimizing the energy required, taking into account the mass and the distance to be transported.
    ⟹⟹ better distance than L2L2 norm between distributions... or than KLKL (the Kullback-Leibler divergence doesn't handle geometry: exact target bin, not around: no notion of neighborhood or distance between bins)
  • Optimal transport formulation: inftransport fieldswmatchingpandq∫x,yw(x,y)C(x,y)dxdyinftransport fieldswmatchingpandq∫x,yw(x,y)C(x,y)dxdy where C(x,y)C(x,y) is the cost of moving some unit mass from point xx to point yy,
    where w(x,y)w(x,y) is the quantity of mass transported from point xx to point yy,
    and where the conditions on the transport ww write:
    • reach the target distribution: ∀y,∫xw(x,y)dx=q(y)∀y,∫xw(x,y)dx=q(y)
    • start from the candidate distribution: ∀x,∫yw(x,y)dx=p(x)∀x,∫yw(x,y)dx=p(x)
      then return the total transportation cost

  • Issue: closed form solution in 1D space, but too expensive to compute in higher dimensional space ⟹⟹ approximation schemes; in particular, approximation by Sinkhorn divergences
    • in both dual/primal formulations: if distributions are sets of points of identical masses (Dirac peaks), then solving the optimal transport problem is equivalent to searching for a bistochastic (*) transport matrix WW minimizing W.∗CW.∗C
      (*) bistochastic = all lines and rows sum up to 1
    • approximation: add a + εentropy(W)εentropy(W) cost, making the problem convex (unique solution)
    • WW is necessarily of the form diag(u)Kdiag(v)diag(u)Kdiag(v), with K=e−C/εK=e−C/ε and only one matrix satisfies this
    • Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm [1967]: WW is obtainable by the iterative algorithm (proved to converge): u./=Kvu./=Kv v./=Kuv./=Ku starting from uu and v=(1111…)v=(1111…) and at convergence return: u(K.∗C)vu(K.∗C)v as ∑ijKijuivj∑ijKijuivj stands for ∫xyw(x,y)dxdy∫xyw(x,y)dxdy and CijCij stands for the transport cost C(x,y)C(x,y).
    • this is implementable with a (fixed in advance) number of simple neural network layers, as all operations are differentiable

  • thus the (approximated) optimal transport cost OT(p,q)OT(p,q) can be used in an optimization process such as backpropagation: minf∑exampleskOT(f(exk),targetqkminf∑exampleskOT(f(exk),targetqk ).

Metric learning: Siamese networks

[Signature Verification using a "Siamese" Time Delay Neural Network; Jane Bromley, Isabelle Guyon, Yann LeCun, Eduard Sickinger and Roopak Shah; NIPS 1994]
  • estimate the best metric (e.g. for some classification task), i.e. for instance, a mapping to another space, in which the Euclidean distance will be relevant
  • if desired distances (between given points) or clusters are known, define a loss on triplets (x,p,n)(x,p,n), such as ∑(x,p,n)max(0,d(x,p)−d(x,n)+m)∑(x,p,n)max(0,d(x,p)−d(x,n)+m) where
    • xx : current sample,
    • pp : positive example : d(x,p)d(x,p) should be small
    • nn : negative example : d(x,n)d(x,n) should be large
    • mm : arbitrary margin
    The optimization of such a loss will push together positive samples with xx and push away negative ones from xx.
    NB : without margin and clamping, the optimization would provably diverge.
  • named Siamese because the network φφ needs to be applied twice (to xx and pp, e.g.) in order to be able to copmute the (Euclidean) distance in the output space (between φ(x)φ(x) and φ(p)φ(p)).

Noisy data (can be seen as noise modeling)

Denoising auto-encoder

[Extracting and composing robust features with denoising autoencoders; Vincent, H. Larochelle Y. Bengio and P.A. Manzagol; ICML 2008]
  • originally meant to robustify auto-encoders (without requiring few middle neurons)
  • get noisy inputs (in the article, artificial additional noise), ask for reconstruction without noise
  • learns to get robust features and to denoise

What if noise is already present in the dataset, but unknown? (no denoised target available)

  • data self-realignment : possible?
  • example: registering RGB satellite images to cadaster maps (binary pictures indicating building presence): no exact ground truth available (spurious deformations in data acquisition & elevation landscape / human mistakes in making cadasters)
    • [Aligning and Updating Cadaster Maps with Aerial Images by Multi-Task, Multi-Resolution Deep Learning; Nicolas Girard, Guillaume Charpiat, Yuliya Tarabalka; ACCV 2018]
    • [Noisy Supervision For Correcting Misaligned Cadaster Maps Without Perfect Ground Truth Data; Nicolas Girard, Guillaume Charpiat, Yuliya Tarabalka; 2019]



  • in the case of a regression task : x↦yx↦y with noisy dataset samples (x,y)(x,y) :
    • if output noise (on yy) is ~i.i.d. (does not depend on x)
      or more exactly: if "relatively similar" x,x′x,x′ do not share more similar noise on y,y′y,y′
      then no bias in the dataset
    • if loss = L2L2 metric : ∑i∥^y(xi)−yi∥2∑i‖y^(xi)−yi‖2 with yi=yirealyi=yireal + unknown noiseinoisei
      then, with the following assumptions:
      • no noise bias (condition above on similar xx)
      • no overfit
      • dense sampling limit (each sample has many neighbors)
      the optimal ^y(xi)y^(xi) is reached at yiyi real!
    • in practice: denoise the dataset this way (here, re-align) and then re-learn from the aligned dataset (as less noise ⟹⟹ better results [theoretically same global optimum, but with different confiedences (= variance)])

Learning a Partial Differential Equation (real equation not known)

  • genetic optimization (of possible terms to incorporate in the equation)
  • a PDE is an iterative process ⟹⟹ model it as a recurrent network
    • PDE = leaky RNN, i.e. not of the type ht+1=f(ht)ht+1=f(ht) but of the form ht+1=ht+f(ht)ht+1=ht+f(ht) : small iterative updates
    • example: segmentation refinement
      [Recurrent Neural Networks to Correct Satellite Image Classification Maps; Emmanuel Maggiori, Guillaume Charpiat, Yuliya Tarabalka and Pierre Alliez; Transactions on Geoscience and Remote Sensing 2016]
      In the spirit of image processing PDEs, learn an iterative process to deblur segmentation maps, using the original RGB image (whose edges might bring useful information)

  • going further: not using a RNN, but using an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) solver
    [Neural Ordinary Differential Equations; Ricky T. Q. Chen, Yulia Rubanova, Jesse Bettencourt, David Duvenaud; best paper at NeurIPS 2018]
    • given fθfθ, which describes the dynamics by dhdt=fθ(ht)dhdt=fθ(ht), and given an initial condition ht0ht0, use an ODE solver to compute htht at any real continuous time t>t0t>t0
      ⟹⟹ more precise than a RNN with discretized time steps
    • the dynamics might depend also on tt, using dhdt=fθ,t(ht)dhdt=fθ,t(ht), for instance using a neural network fθfθ with two inputs: tt and htht
    • given a loss to be minimized L(hT)L(hT) with respect to the dynamics fθ,tfθ,t, one can similarly use an ODE solver to compute ∇θL(hT)∇θL(hT):
      • define the "adjoint" a(t)=∂L∂hta(t)=∂L∂ht, which tells how the state htht should change at time tt to decrease the loss
      • using the chain rule (as for backpropagation), this adjoint satisfies: da(t)dt=−a(t)⊤∂fθ,t(ht)∂htda(t)dt=−a(t)⊤∂fθ,t(ht)∂ht
      • and then the total variation with respect to the parameters θθ can be expressed by integration: dLdθ=−∫t0t1a(t)⊤∂fθ,t(ht)∂θdtdLdθ=−∫t1t0a(t)⊤∂fθ,t(ht)∂θdt
      • this integral can be computed using an ODE solver...
    • thus one can learn the dynamics fθ,tfθ,t
    • example: complete a temporal series (x0,x1,x2…xN)(x0,x1,x2…xN):
      • consider that the dynamics learned / to be learned do not act directly on xtxt but on an internal state htht (in order to be able to deal with partial or noisy information, etc.)
      • so first, transform this sequence into a guessed initial internal state ^ht=0h^t=0 (named z0z0 in the figure below), using an RNN (going reverse in time, from xNxN to x0x0; the RNN has to be learned as well)
      • then apply the dynamics fθ,tfθ,t previously learned, to ^h0h^0 (using an ODE solver)
      • this gives guessed states ^hth^t for all times, in particular ^ht>Nh^t>N
      • from them, deduce missing observations ^xt>Nx^t>N

Knowing an equation that the solution has to satisfy : solving PDEs!

[DGM: A deep learning algorithm for solving partial differential equations; Justin Sirignano and Konstantinos Spiliopoulos; Journal of Computational Physics 2018]
  • equation to solve, of the form:
    ∂tu(t,x)+Lu(t,x)=0∀t,x∈[0,T]×Ω∂tu(t,x)+Lu(t,x)=0∀t,x∈[0,T]×Ω
    with constraints u(0,x)=u0(x)∀x∈Ωu(0,x)=u0(x)∀x∈Ω [initial condition]
    and u(t,x)=g(t,x)∀x∈[0,T]×∂Ωu(t,x)=g(t,x)∀x∈[0,T]×∂Ω [boundary condition: control]

    where:
    • one searches for a function uu defined over a spatio-temporal domain Ω×[0,T]Ω×[0,T]
    • LL is a differential operator, not necessarily linear, applied to function uu
    • ∂x∂x denotes the partial derivative with respect to xx
    • ∂Ω∂Ω denotes the boundary of domain ΩΩ

  • minimize J(f)=∥∂tf+Lf∥2[0,T]×Ω+∥f−g∥2[0,T]×∂Ω+∥f(0,.)−u0∥2ΩJ(f)=‖∂tf+Lf‖[0,T]×Ω2+‖f−g‖[0,T]×∂Ω2+‖f(0,.)−u0‖Ω2
  • operators such as ∂t∂t or LL are easy to implement on top of the network providing ff
  • proved (under assumptions) and checked experimentally to converge to 0 (and f→f→ solutions)
  • in practice: replace integral with Monte Carlo estimates (i.e. Ebatch samplesEbatch samples ...) and replace second-order derivatives ∂x1∂x2f(t,x)∂x1∂x2f(t,x) with approximations of type ∂xf(t,x+ε)−∂xf(t,x)ε∂xf(t,x+ε)−∂xf(t,x)ε.

Important example in chemistry

[Machine Learning of coarse-grained Molecular Dynamics Force Fields; Jiang Wang, Christoph Wehmeyer, Frank Noe', Cecilia Clementi]
  • Finding or approximating quantum mechanics equations' solutions is a very important topic in chemistry, as these equations are hard to solve (very heavy computations) and molecule properties depend on them
  • (energy) potential function U(x)U(x) around a molecule xx: hard to model / compute
  • with ML : train x↦U(x)x↦U(x) from simulations
  • desired physical property: −∂xU(x)=ForceField(x)−∂xU(x)=ForceField(x)
  • minimize ∥∂xU(x,θ)+ForceField(x)∥2‖∂xU(x,θ)+ForceField(x)‖2 w.r.t. θθ (parameters of the neural network standing for UU)

Form of the solution known

[Deep Learning for Physical Processes: Incorporating Prior Scientific Knowledge; Emmanuel de Bezenac, Arthur Pajot, Patrick Gallinari; 2017]
  • goal: prediction of the evolution of temperature maps (sea surface temperature, from satellite imagery)
  • equation satisfied by the fluid (the ocean streams): advection-diffusion: ∂tI+w⋅∂xI=D∂2xxI∂tI+w⋅∂xI=D∂xx2I (∂2xx∂xx2 = Laplacian, DD = diffusion constant, ww = unknown motion field)
  • theorem: the solution of this equation is of the form: I(x,t)=∫R214πDte−∥x−y−w∥24DtI0(y)dyI(x,t)=∫R214πDte−‖x−y−w‖24DtI0(y)dy
  • instead of predicting directly the new state at t+1t+1 by learning I(.,t)↦I(.,t+1)I(.,t)↦I(.,t+1) directly, learn to predict the motion: I(.,t)↦w(.,t)I(.,t)↦w(.,t) from which one can estimate I(.,t+1)I(.,t+1)
  • criterion to optimize: good prediction of I(t+1)I(t+1) + regularization of ww (smooth, small, small divergence)
         

    ⟹⟹ better results than direct prediction; this said, the same team, in a later paper, without using the solution form, but using an ODE solver, obtained better results [Learning Dynamical Systems from Partial Observations; Ibrahim Ayed, Emmanuel de Bézenac, Arthur Pajot, Julien Brajard, Patrick Gallinari; 2019]

Consider a dynamical system:
  • xt+1=F(xt)xt+1=F(xt) with fixed, unknown function FF, and xt∈Rdxt∈Rd
  • if FF is linear: "easy" (or, at least, a classic problem) to retrieve from data samples (xt,xt+1,…)(xt,xt+1,…) as a mean square problem ∑samplesx∥xt+1−Axt∥2∑samplesx‖xt+1−Axt‖2
  • solution: note X=[x1,x2,...xT]X=[x1,x2,...xT]
    and Y=[x2,x3,...xT+1]Y=[x2,x3,...xT+1]
    then the matrix YX†→YX†→ optimal AA when TT increases
    where X†X† is the Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse of XX, which is limε→0(X∗X+εId)−1Xlimε→0(X∗X+εId)−1X
  • what if FF non linear?

Koopman - von Neumann classical mechanics using a quantum mechanics formalism:
  • Theorem: there exists a representation space in which any dynamical system becomes linear
    ⟹⟹ approximate this representation with a neural network! and learn linear dynamics there
  • unfortunately: this representation space = a complex Hilbert space (i.e., infinite-dimensioned vector space)

[Deep Dynamical Modeling and Control of Unsteady Fluid Flows; Jeremy Morton, Freddie D. Witherden, Antony Jameson, Mykel J. Kochenderfer; NIPS 2018]

    X→X′X→X′
    Y→Y′Y→Y′
    mapping to another space,
    such that the dynamics in that space are linear, i.e. Y′=A′X′Y′=A′X′ for some matrix A′A′
    and the inverse network h=g−1:h=g−1:
    X′→XX′→X
    Y′→YY′→Y
    .
    Then, for new XX, predict h(A′(g(X)))h(A′(g(X))) [supposed to be close to YY]
